IPT-SP study

AIM

To assess the prevalence, risk factors and health outcomes of SP resistance in IPT for malaria among ANC attendees in Ilishan-Remo, Nigeria.

Specific objectives:

↠ determine the prevalence of SP molecular markers in the parasites from pregnant women
↠ explore potential epidemiological factors affecting the prevalence of SP resistance molecular markers in the pregnant women.
↠ assess if there are any notable differences between peripheral and placental parasites in the profile of SP resistance molecular markers
↠ assess maternal, fetal and neonatal outcomes among women with IPTp-SP resistance

Study design

↠ A prospective cohort study of 500 pregnant women
↠ Study duration: 24 months

Primary Outcome

↠ Prevalence of resistant mutant gene markers for SP

Secondary Outcome

↠ Prevalence of peripheral parasitemia at delivery
↠ Prevalence of placental parasitemia (any method) at delivery •Incidence of maternal and newborn morbidity (composite of: preterm delivery, low birth weight, stillbirth)
↠ Parasitemia (prevalence and density) :pre-IPTp and day 28 post-IPTp
↠ Risk factors for SP resistance
↠ Hemoglobin changes at delivery

Study status: Currently at 91% enrolment (458 subjects)

Funder: European and Developing countries Clinical trials Partnership (EDCTP)

Atinuke’s profile

Dr. Atinuke Olaleye is the Director of the Centre for Advanced Medical Research and Biotechnology (CAMRAB), Babcock University,
Nigeria where she coordinates clinical research studies as well as research capacity-building activities for early-career researchers.
She is a board-certified Obstetrician Gynecologist, an EDCTP Career Development Fellow and a WHO-TDR Clinical Research and Development Fellow.
Dr Olaleye is a Clinical researcher, as well as a Senior Lecturer and Consultant Obstetrician Gynecologist at the Babcock University and Teaching Hospital, Ogun state, Nigeria.
